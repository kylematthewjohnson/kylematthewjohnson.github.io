Dance, meet Data¶

Prior to attending the University of Washington and studying computer science/data science, I worked as the Marketing Director of City Opera Ballet. During my time there I often struggled to glean valuable insights from the ticket sales reports generated by BrownPaperTickets. Once I started my studies at UW I thought it would be a fun challenge to take another look at this data.

The companies ticket sales reports were in a series of excel files by show. Upon looking at the data, I found several key issues that needed to be addressed in order to get anything useful from these reports.

Cleanup Time¶

Now onto a bit of cleaning. To begin with, the excel files are filled with empty rows, and header/title rows that are of little use. There are also multiple duplicate header rows in each file associated with different delivery methods. These all needed to be dropped from the DataFrames.

I also chose to drop all rows with a price of $0 (complementary tickets) as none of these tickets had customer data associated with them.

In [3]:
for k,v in data.items():
    # drop any lines with fewer than 3 values
    v.dropna(axis=0, how='any', thresh=3, inplace=True)
    # drop any duplicate lines
    v.drop_duplicates(keep=False, inplace=True)
    # drop all lines where Price==0 (complementary tickets)
    v.query('Price != 0', inplace=True)
    # reset the index column
    v.index = np.arange(1, len(v)+1)

These DataFrames also contained several columns of data that wasn't useful for the analysis we were interested in. So I dropped those and renamed the other columns with more concise/useful names.

Another major issue that was making any useful analysis difficult was the inconsistency in the "Admission Level" across different shows. All in all I found a total of 54 separate admission levels between the shows. I condensed these down to 7 to make it easier to analyze audience demographics.

Next was the issue of zipcodes. Most of the zipcodes in the original excel file were stored in numerical format, and so the zipcodes for cities like Bar Harbor, ME, with zipcodes 04609 had the initial 0 dropped in the excel sheet.

There were also some customers who entered there zipcode in the 9-digit format. I trimmed off the trailing 4 digits from the 9-digit zipcodes and added the leading zeroes to the zipcodes beginning in 0.

In [6]:
for k,v in data.items():
    # cast ZIP as a string
    v['ZIP'] = v['ZIP'].astype('str', copy=False)
    # trim trailing digits of 9-digit zipcodes
    v['ZIP'] = v['ZIP'].str[:5]
    # add leading zeroes to zipcodes starting with 0 (e.g. 04609, Bar Harbor, ME)
    v['ZIP'] = v['ZIP'].apply(lambda x: '{0:0>5}'.format(x))

master = pd.DataFrame(columns=['Show','Revenue','Attendance'])
